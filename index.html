Abstract

We calibrate a camera to a LiDAR by working entirely in a 2D projective LiDAR render. Our core observation is that when the LiDAR point cloud is rendered with a virtual pinhole camera placed exactly at the center of the LiDAR—its optical “eye,” which itself requires calibrating—the resulting 2D projective map is occlusion-free: every LiDAR return maps to a unique pixel with no self-occlusion or depth ordering ambiguity. This motivates us to define all calibration objectives in this occlusion-free 2D projective LiDAR render from the virtual LiDAR-eye camera, rather than in the LiDAR's native angular coordinates where a wide field-of-view sensor wraps at the panorama seam and bends straight target edges into curves. Formulating the plane-attract / silhouette-repel objective in the LiDAR-eye render makes it exact and wrap-safe, which yields an objective landscape that is far easier to drive to global optimality than prior feature-correspondence formulations. Crucially, this same formulation uniquely admits a multi-frame objective: because every frame is expressed in one consistent, occlusion-free LiDAR-eye view, we jointly optimize a single extrinsic across all frames without any per-frame heuristic feature estimation—a multi-frame objective that prior target-based methods could not reach. On a wide-FoV Ouster OS-1-128 plus rolling-shutter camera rig, our fast closed-form initializer and differentiable optimizer converge to the same extrinsic, and dense overlays of the calibrated cloud align well across the full panorama.

Method

Three stages, all expressed in the eye of the LiDAR.

1

Calibrate the LiDAR to its own eye

The reported sensor origin is not the true ray origin. We recover the LiDAR optical-center offset δc by minimizing the variance of nearest-neighbor azimuthal gaps—i.e., finding the center that makes the angular spacing most uniform. The animation sweeps δc and shows the range panorama sharpening into a consistent angular grid.

2

Visualize the calibration objective

We place a yaw-aligned virtual pinhole camera at the recovered eye and optimize a two-threshold binary objective: attract on-plane, in-silhouette returns toward the board plane, and repel the projected board edge (yellow polygon) past clearly off-plane returns, while abstaining on ambiguous near-plane points. The animation shows the board silhouette converging over optimization steps as the alignment score improves. Because projection is linear, the silhouette stays a straight-edged polygon and the objective is exact and wrap-safe.

3

Calibrated result & depth-render quality

With the extrinsic recovered, we project the calibrated cloud onto every RGB frame and render the LiDAR range panorama. The dense overlay stays aligned across the full wide field of view, including across the panorama seam. Our fast closed-form initializer and the differentiable optimizer agree to within ∼2 cm in translation.
