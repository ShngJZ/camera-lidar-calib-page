Abstract

We present a target-based camera–LiDAR extrinsic calibration method that performs the geometric alignment in the eye of the LiDAR. Target-based calibration typically establishes sparse feature correspondences (hole centers, corners) and expresses the objective in the LiDAR's angular coordinates, where two problems arise on wide field-of-view sensors: the azimuth wraps at ±π, tearing a target near the seam into two, and spherical projection bends straight board edges into curves, so a polygon silhouette objective is only approximate. Our key insight is to recover the LiDAR optical center (its “eye”), place a fixed yaw-aligned virtual pinhole camera at that center, and express a plane-attract / silhouette-repel objective in that virtual camera's pixel space. Because pinhole projection is linear in homogeneous coordinates, straight board edges map to straight image edges—making the polygon signed-distance objective exact—and points that straddle the panorama seam land in adjacent pixels, so the formulation is wrap-safe by construction. On a wide-FoV Ouster OS-1-128 plus rolling-shutter camera rig, our fast closed-form initializer and differentiable optimizer converge to the same extrinsic within ∼2 cm, and dense overlays of the calibrated cloud align well across the full panorama.

Method

Three stages, all expressed in the eye of the LiDAR.

1

Calibrate the LiDAR to its own eye

The reported sensor origin is not the true ray origin. We recover the LiDAR optical-center offset δc by minimizing the variance of nearest-neighbor azimuthal gaps—i.e., finding the center that makes the angular spacing most uniform. The animation sweeps δc and shows the range panorama sharpening into a consistent angular grid.

2

Visualize the calibration objective

We place a yaw-aligned virtual pinhole camera at the recovered eye and optimize a two-threshold binary objective: attract on-plane, in-silhouette returns toward the board plane, and repel the projected board edge (yellow polygon) past clearly off-plane returns, while abstaining on ambiguous near-plane points. The animation shows the board silhouette converging over optimization steps as the alignment score improves. Because projection is linear, the silhouette stays a straight-edged polygon and the objective is exact and wrap-safe.

3

Calibrated result & depth-render quality

With the extrinsic recovered, we project the calibrated cloud onto every RGB frame and render the LiDAR range panorama. The dense overlay stays aligned across the full wide field of view, including across the panorama seam. Our fast closed-form initializer and the differentiable optimizer agree to within ∼2 cm in translation.

Self-Consistency

We report calibration quality as self-consistency on a single wide-FoV session, not against an external ground-truth extrinsic.

  • Sensor rig. Ouster OS-1-128 (1024×10, wide FoV) + rolling-shutter camera (1920×1080/eye).
  • Recovered eye offset. δc = [0.4, 0.1, −30.7] mm, consistent with the sensor's LiDAR-to-origin z offset.
  • Init vs. refine. Closed-form IRLS + consensus initializer agrees with the Adam optimizer to ∼2 cm in translation.
  • Board-plane residuals. Median ∼6.5–19.3 mm across frames (self-consistency proxy).
